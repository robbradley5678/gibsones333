The Gibson ES-333 was launched in 2003 as a more affordable verion of the ES-335. Visually, it was stripped back, with a thin satin finish, a silkscreen headstock logo with no inlays, and no pickup covers or pickguard. Unlike the newer ES-335 studio model, it keeps the body and fingerboard binding.

Intead of the typical Classic 57 pickups, the ES-333 used the 490R and 498T in the neck and bridge positions. Gibson used these pickups in a lot of Les Paul models during this period. They have have a higher output than the 57's, giving the ES-333 a brighter and hotter tone when compared to a typical ES-335. It retains the standard 3-way selector switch and two-volume, two-tone controls.

One interesting feature is the access pannel in the back of the body, compared to the solid rear of the ES-335. This was carried over from the ES-335 studio of the 1990s, and made repairs and modifications to the electronics much easier.

At the time, stores were selling these guitars for hundreds of dollars, rather than thousands, and represented a great deal. The cream binding, thin finish and mint-green tuner heads gave the guitar the look and feel of a vintage instrument, and the Les Paul pickups really packed a punch.

Sadly the ES-333 was discontinued in 2005, giving way to the ES-335 studio which was reissued in 2012. Despite their short production time, the ES-333 sold well, and used examples can be found fairly easily.

In recent years their values have increased, but they are still more affordable then the ES-335, and still represent a bargain guitar.

Colors

The ES-333 was finished in satin nitrocellulose, available in four colors: Cherry Red, Sunburst, Faded Brown, and Natural.

Modifications

With the addition of a pickguard, nickel pickup covers and top-hat control knobs, the ES-333 can be to look like an ES-335. The cream binding, thin finish and green tuners lend themselves very well towards a vintage look. The satin finish can be polished with a fine cutting compound, and buffed to a mirror shine with an automotive wax.

(Pro tip: using wax on the back of the neck eliminates the stickiness of the nitro finish. You'll have to reapply it fairly frequently though!)

Check out this ES-333 next to a 1966 ES-335. Looks pretty good, doesn't it?

The rear access pannel makes changing the pickups much easier. The 490 and 498's can be too hot for some player's tastes, and swapping to Classic 57's is a common modification. Using pure nickel strings can help tone them down a little.

Buy an ES-333

Despite their short production run, the ES-333 was a popular guitar and can often be found for sale on online. The majority are in the USA, but Gibson sold them worldwide, so finding one nearby is just a matter of time.

The best places to look would be Reverb and eBay, but keep your eye on forums and social media groups, and even your local music store - you never know when one will pop up.

Prices have varied $800 to $1400 range, depeding on their condition and location.
